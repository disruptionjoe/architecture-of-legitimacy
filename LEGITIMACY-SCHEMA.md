# Legitimacy Schema

This project treats legitimacy as a first-class design variable.

## Provisional Definition

A contribution institution is legitimate to the extent that informed participants continue to choose participation because they regard its rules, judgments, rights, and adaptation pathways as acceptably fair, contestable, and non-coercive.

This is a working definition, not doctrine.

## Legitimacy Is Not Correctness

Valuation can be wrong while process remains legitimate.

A system may undervalue a contribution and still retain legitimacy if:

- the basis for the judgment is visible,
- the contributor can contest it,
- the rules can be updated,
- the contributor can exit without erasure,
- and the system learns from the failure.

A system may value well while remaining illegitimate if:

- decisions are opaque,
- appeal is impossible,
- exits destroy earned value,
- rule changes are captured,
- or participation is effectively coerced.

## Proposed Legitimacy Conditions

### 1. Visibility

Participants should be able to see the rules, rubrics, decision process, and contribution record.

Failure mode:

Evaluation feels arbitrary because the process is not inspectable.

### 2. Contestability

Participants should have a way to challenge classifications, rubrics, or governance decisions.

Failure mode:

The system becomes procedurally neat but substantively unchallengeable.

### 3. Voice

Participants should be able to propose changes or give structured feedback on rules that affect them.

Failure mode:

The project listens socially but never changes structurally.

### 4. Exit With Retained Record

Participants should be able to leave without losing the record of accepted contributions.

Failure mode:

Exit becomes coercive because leaving forfeits all future claim or recognition.

### 5. Adaptive Rule Change

The system should improve rules when evidence shows they are failing.

Failure mode:

Legibility hardens into bureaucracy.

### 6. Bounded Authority

Founder or reviewer powers should be named and constrained over time.

Failure mode:

Progressive decentralization becomes theater.

### 7. Non-Capture

The system should become harder to capture as legitimacy and participation grow.

Failure mode:

The project's success creates a larger prize for attackers without increasing defense.

## Legitimacy Metrics To Explore

Possible qualitative and quantitative indicators:

- contributor retention after adverse decisions,
- dispute rate by contribution type,
- reversal rate after appeal,
- time to review,
- percentage of decisions with explicit rationale,
- rule changes triggered by participant feedback,
- contributor perception of fairness,
- concentration of decision power,
- concentration of accepted value,
- and capture-risk indicators.

These metrics can be gamed. They are research instruments, not replacements for judgment.

## Open Questions

1. Which legitimacy conditions are necessary from day one?
2. Which conditions can emerge later without damaging trust?
3. Can legitimacy be modeled as an opt-in stability condition?
4. How should legitimacy failures be classified?
5. How can a system distinguish legitimate dissatisfaction from adversarial pressure?
