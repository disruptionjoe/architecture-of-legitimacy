## Source-Supported Notes

- Academic peer review uses expert assessment of submitted manuscripts as part
  of journal editorial decision-making.
- Editors may reject manuscripts without external review and are not bound to
  follow reviewer recommendations.
- Journals vary in whether review is open, anonymized, single-anonymized,
  double-anonymized, or accompanied by published peer-review history.
- Confidentiality is central in many pre-publication review processes; reviewer
  comments and identities are not necessarily public.
- Some journals publish reviewer comments, author responses, and decision
  letters after acceptance through transparent or opt-in peer-review history
  systems.
- Appeal paths can exist, but at least the PLOS ONE model limits appeals to
  narrow conditions such as major factual error or compromised objectivity.
- Sources name live peer-review failure modes: disputed value, bias, lack of
  transparency, reviewer disagreement, missed errors, slow review cycles,
  conflicts of interest, predatory journal claims, and limits on fraud
  detection.

## Directly Supported Versus Inferred

Directly supported by the packet sources:

- Peer review is expert assessment used by editors in manuscript decisions.
- Editors remain responsible for content selection and may choose not to send a
  manuscript for external review.
- Review models vary by journal, including anonymous, double-anonymized,
  transparent, and opt-in peer-review-history systems.
- Confidentiality, conflict disclosure, reviewer conduct, process transparency,
  appeals, and post-publication critique are named parts of peer-review
  governance.
- Peer-review value and reliability are debated, with named concerns about
  bias, missed errors, opacity, reviewer disagreement, incentives, and speed.

Reviewer inferences requiring later review:

- Academic peer review likely overlaps C2/C3 around validation path, bounded
  authority, record trace, and post-publication contestability.
- The overlap may be strongest for knowledge legitimacy and weakest for
  retained value, reward timing, founder-transition design, and public
  contribution-accounting granularity.
- The repo's differentiator may be an explicit public trace from local
  contribution evidence through accepted record, loss notes, retained value,
  governance transition, and capture monitoring rather than review itself.
- Peer review may also be a failure-warning source: expert validation can remain
  prestigious while still being slow, opaque, biased, inconsistent, or weakly
  contestable.
