## Source-Supported Notes

- Wikipedia is a collaborative encyclopedia project with community-developed
  policies, guidelines, and norms.
- Consensus is the primary decision method for many editorial and policy
  decisions, and it is not identical to vote counting.
- Ordinary disputes can move through editing, talk-page discussion, outside
  input, dispute-resolution venues, and conduct-focused escalation.
- Arbitration Committee requests are available for serious conduct disputes
  when community resolution has failed.
- Page histories preserve sequences of edits and revision comparison, making a
  contributor record reconstructable at page level.
- Wikipedia's content-ownership policy rejects individual ownership of article
  content, including by creators or article subjects.
- Wikipedia names systemic bias, sockpuppetry, and paid-contribution disclosure
  as live problems or risk surfaces.

## Directly Supported Versus Inferred

Directly supported by the packet sources:

- Wikipedia uses community-developed policies, guidelines, consensus, and dispute-resolution processes.
- Wikipedia preserves page-level edit histories and revision comparisons.
- Wikipedia rejects individual ownership of article content.
- Wikipedia names systemic bias, sockpuppetry, and paid-contribution disclosure as governance or capture concerns.
- ArbCom escalation exists for serious conduct disputes not resolved by ordinary community processes.

Reviewer inferences requiring later review:

- Wikipedia may overlap C2/C3 because it couples public contribution records,
  legitimacy conditions, dispute handling, anti-abuse controls, and distributed
  governance.
- The likely overlap may be strongest around record trace, contestability, and
  governance, but this packet does not assign a collision level.
- Wikipedia appears weaker as a comparison for reward or retained-value rights
  because individual contributors do not own accepted text and the source set
  does not show a native reward-allocation mechanism.
- This repo's differentiator may be contribution-to-reward or retained-value
  design, not public contribution legitimacy by itself.
