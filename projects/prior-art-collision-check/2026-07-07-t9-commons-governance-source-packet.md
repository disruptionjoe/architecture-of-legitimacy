## Source-Supported Notes

- The commons-governance literature studies institutions for common-pool
  resources, not merely generic collaboration or voluntary community norms.
- Ostrom's design-principle frame emphasizes durable rule systems developed by
  resource users or participants, with attention to boundaries, rule fit,
  collective choice, monitoring, sanctions, conflict resolution, recognized
  local rulemaking authority, and nested governance.
- The IAD framework provides a reusable way to analyze action situations,
  participants, rules, interactions, outcomes, and evaluation.
- Ostrom's Nobel lecture describes a research program that compared organized
  and failed common-pool resource cases rather than assuming only private
  ownership or state control could solve overuse.
- The design-principles literature is empirical and comparative: it asks which
  institutional conditions make long-term collective action more likely.
- Cox, Arnold, and Villamayor Tomas found broad empirical support for the
  principles across many studies, while warning that they are incomplete,
  contingent, and scale-sensitive.
- The source set is strong on legitimacy conditions, bounded participant
  authority, monitoring, local rule revision, sanctions, conflict resolution,
  and nested governance.
- The source set is weaker for this repo's contribution-record problem because
  common-pool resource governance is not automatically a contribution-log,
  retained-value, reward-readiness, or public research-pilot workflow.

## Directly Supported Versus Inferred

Directly supported by the packet sources:

- Ostrom's design principles are presented as factors associated with robust
  common-pool resource institutions.
- The principles address boundaries, rule fit, collective choice, monitoring,
  sanctions, conflict resolution, recognized local rulemaking rights, and nested
  governance.
- The IAD framework analyzes institutional action situations using actors,
  rules, interactions, outcomes, and evaluation.
- The commons-governance literature compares empirical cases rather than only
  assuming state or private ownership as the available solutions.
- Cox, Arnold, and Villamayor Tomas treat the principles as empirically robust
  but not complete or deterministic.

Reviewer inferences requiring later review:

- Commons governance may be the strongest pressure so far on C2/C3/C6 for
  legitimacy conditions and bounded authority.
- The local repo's differentiator may be the attempt to bind commons-like
  legitimacy conditions to visible contribution records, retained-value
  ambiguity, reward-readiness gating, and a small public research workflow.
- The commons lane may be weaker on reward timing and contribution-level record
  trace than on institutional legitimacy.
- A later verdict-bearing review should probably compare one concrete commons
  case or coding framework before assigning any collision level.
