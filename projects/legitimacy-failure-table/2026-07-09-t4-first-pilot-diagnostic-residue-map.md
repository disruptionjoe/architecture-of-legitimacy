## Screening Question

```text
After a reviewer diagnoses a legitimacy failure in a later first-pilot packet,
what must remain visible so the diagnosis can be reviewed, repaired, contested,
or stopped without private founder context?
```

The map assumes no real contributor, target, source set, reviewer, issue, pull

selected.

## Residue Model

Diagnostic residue is the inspectable remainder of a legitimacy diagnosis. It
is not the fix itself. It is what a later reviewer would need in order to see
why a failure was named, which response lane was chosen, and which consequences
were deliberately blocked.

| residue field | what it preserves | safe current output | stop boundary |
|---|---|---|---|
| Failure signal | The observed public fact that triggered T4 review. | A synthetic note naming the missing, conflicting, or overloaded field. | Do not infer motives, sanctions, eligibility limits, or bad faith from the signal alone. |
| Condition at risk | Which legitimacy condition appears stressed first. | A provisional condition label such as visibility, contestability, voice, exit, bounded authority, adaptive rule change, or non-capture. | Do not change legitimacy conditions or mark T4 passed. |
| Source/inference split | Which facts are source-backed and which are reviewer interpretation. | A source-backed fact list plus a separate inference note. | Do not fill missing source facts, choose a source lane, or create a novelty verdict. |
| Response lane | Whether the first response is local repair, evidence gathering, Joe review, or governance/capture stop. | A lane label from the response-routing map. | Do not convert the lane into active policy or reviewer authority. |
| Local repair delta | What would make the packet more inspectable without changing rules. | A bounded missing-field, rationale, context-link, loss-note, or source-trace repair. | Do not edit templates, contribution standards, review policy, or live records. |
| Useful residue | What remains valuable if the packet is revised, narrowed, or not accepted. | A loss-note or retained-record prompt. | Do not create retained-value rights, reward expectations, acceptance, or legal meaning. |
| Contestable point | What a later participant could challenge without gaining appeal rights by implication. | A challengeable fact, inference, or classification boundary. | Do not create appeal deadlines, reviewer obligations, dispute process, or response-time promises. |
| Protected non-conclusion | Which meanings must not be inferred from the diagnostic label. | Explicit no-policy, no-status, no-live-record, no-right, and no-reward language. | Do not soften these into adoption language. |
| Next authorization | The next decision that would require Joe or governed review. | A review question, not an action. | Do not perform the authorized action during unattended Progress. |

## First-Pilot Diagnostic Application

The current synthetic first-pilot packet stack is review-prep material, not a
live contribution surface. The diagnostic residue map therefore treats every
later T4 finding as a candidate review note until the packet has a real target,
source set, contributor, reviewer, and rationale.

For a later filled packet, reviewers should preserve:

1. The exact field, source, rationale, or state that triggered the T4 diagnosis.
2. The affected T4 failure mode and the primary legitimacy condition at risk.
3. The public facts available before reviewer inference.
4. The response lane chosen and why a stronger lane was not used.
5. The local repair that could improve inspectability without changing rules.
6. The useful residue preserved if the packet is narrowed or not accepted.
7. The protected meanings excluded from the diagnosis.
8. The next decision that requires Joe or governed review before adoption.

If those eight points cannot be recorded from public packet materials, the
right current state is `needs_revision`, `needs_public_context`, or
`governed_review_required`, not `review_ready`.

## Residue Completeness Check

A diagnostic note is minimally complete when a later reviewer can answer:

1. What public fact created the legitimacy concern?
2. Which part of the diagnosis is source-backed?
3. Which part is reviewer inference?
4. Which legitimacy condition is at risk first?
5. Which T4 failure mode is the closest fit?
6. Which response lane is selected?
7. What local repair is allowed without policy movement?
8. What useful residue survives a non-acceptance or revision?
9. What consequence is explicitly blocked?
10. What later authorization would be needed to act beyond review prep?

This check is intentionally stricter than a normal packet field-completeness
check. It asks whether the diagnosis itself remains legitimate and inspectable.
