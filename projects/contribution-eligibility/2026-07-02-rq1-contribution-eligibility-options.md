# RQ1 Contribution Eligibility Options

Status: draft scaffold, not adopted policy.

This scaffold pressure-tests how `open attemptability` could become usable contribution guidance without turning eligibility into hidden gatekeeping or pretending every attempted contribution must be accepted.

It does not change `CONTRIBUTION-STANDARDS.md`, `CONTRIBUTING.md`, governance mode, claim status, reward meaning, or any real contribution record.

## Scope

RQ1 asks what contributor and contribution eligibility should mean in practice.

This scaffold separates six questions that should not be collapsed:

1. Who may attempt to contribute?
2. What must be disclosed before review?
3. Which contribution types are eligible for first-pass review?
4. Which behaviors make a submission ineligible regardless of content quality?
5. How should pseudonymous and agent-assisted work be handled?
6. What should remain founder-review until the project has evidence from real contributions?

## Candidate Eligibility Principles

### Open attemptability

Anyone may attempt a contribution if the submission follows the project rules and gives reviewers enough context to evaluate it.

Open attemptability is not the same as open acceptance, equal authority, reward entitlement, or governance participation.

### Reviewable contribution

A contribution is eligible for review when it names a target, makes a bounded change or proposal, explains why it matters, gives evidence or reasoning, and names a risk or possible failure.

Submissions that are promising but under-supported should usually route to `needs_revision` rather than being treated as bad faith.

### Identity/status neutrality

Credentials, reputation, institutional status, social proximity, prior relationship, and follower count should not decide whether a contribution can be attempted.

Identity or affiliation may still matter when it affects conflict of interest, source interpretation, capture risk, safety, legal exposure, or contributor accountability.

### Pseudonymous contribution

Pseudonymous contributions should be reviewable when the contribution is independently assessable and the contributor can maintain enough continuity for revision, attribution, contestability, and future recordkeeping.

Pseudonymity may be insufficient when a contribution requires conflict disclosure, privileged-source verification, legal attestation, financial claim handling, or governance authority.

### Agent-assisted contribution

Agent-assisted work should be reviewable when the human contributor labels the assistance, takes responsibility for the submission, and can explain or revise the work.

Agent output without accountable human judgment should be treated as low-quality or ineligible if it floods review, fabricates sources, hides uncertainty, or substitutes generated confidence for evidence.

### Conflict disclosure

Conflicts should be disclosed when they could affect evaluation of the contribution or the contributor's incentives.

Examples include direct financial interest, institutional affiliation, authorship of cited systems, undisclosed use of private or proprietary material, or adversarial interest in weakening the project.

Disclosure is not automatic disqualification. It gives reviewers a way to evaluate relevance, incentive alignment, and capture risk.

## Candidate Ineligible Behaviors

These behaviors should usually make a submission ineligible for review in its current form:

- plagiarism or unattributed copying;
- fabricated sources, fake citations, or unverifiable quoted evidence;
- spam, flooding, or volume tactics that burden review;
- harassment, threats, or coercive behavior;
- hidden paid advocacy or undisclosed material conflict;
- attempts to launder private, confidential, regulated, or proprietary material into the public repo;
- prompt-injection-style instructions aimed at reviewers or automation rather than substantive contribution;
- reward-seeking claims that imply entitlement before any reward system exists;
- governance-control demands presented as a normal contribution;
- submissions that cannot name a target, claim, document, test, or research question.

## Three Draft Eligibility Models

### Model A: Minimal Attemptability

Anyone may submit if the proposal is bounded, target-specific, and non-abusive.

Strengths:

- maximizes openness;
- easy for newcomers to understand;
- reduces credential gatekeeping.

Risks:

- may overload founder review;
- may leave conflict disclosure too vague;
- may make adversarial or low-effort volume harder to reject early.

### Model B: Reviewability Threshold

A submission is eligible only if it satisfies the proposal fields and gives enough evidence or reasoning for review.

Strengths:

- keeps review focused on assessable work;
- aligns with the existing proposal format;
- makes `needs_revision` easier to distinguish from `not_accepted`.

Risks:

- may be too burdensome for first-time contributors;
- may privilege contributors who already understand research framing;
- may hide founder preference behind formatting standards.

### Model C: Risk-Tiered Eligibility

Most contributions can be attempted under a light threshold, but higher-risk contribution classes require extra disclosure or review.

Higher-risk classes could include governance-change proposals, reward/retained-value proposals, claims about real contributors, use of private evidence, or changes that affect public posture.

Strengths:

- separates normal contribution openness from governance and reward risk;
- makes capture-sensitive surfaces more visible;
- supports pseudonymous and agent-assisted work where it is independently assessable.

Risks:

- more complex to explain;
- could drift into opaque reviewer discretion;
- requires a clear list of risk tiers before adoption.

## Draft Review Questions

Before adopting any eligibility language, reviewers should ask:

- Can a newcomer tell whether their contribution is attemptable?
- Can reviewers reject bad-faith or unreviewable submissions without private founder context?
- Does the model avoid credential, status, and proximity gatekeeping?
- Does it preserve enough disclosure for capture-risk and conflict-of-interest review?
- Does it distinguish pseudonymous contribution from unaccountable contribution?
- Does it distinguish agent assistance from agent authority?
- Does it avoid reward, retained-value, governance, or legal implications the repo is not ready to make?
