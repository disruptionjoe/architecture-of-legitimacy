## Review Question

The second ring should not be reviewed as five independent policy tracks.

The practical review question is:

```text
Which second-ring decision would become unsafe, overclaimed, or incoherent
unless another research gate has already produced visible evidence?
```

The answer matters because reward, rights, governance transfer, capture
response, AI authority, and transfer claims can each turn a local research
artifact into an institutional commitment if the boundaries blur.

## What Can Still Progress Locally

Safe second-ring progress should mostly create better review packets, not
stronger commitments.

Good local work includes:

- turning one synthetic contribution trace into a governance-transition evidence
  packet without moving any power;
- mapping one exit or identity-change scenario without granting rights;
- taking one attack pattern and naming the local repair versus escalation
  threshold;
- documenting one AI-assisted review trace with human rationale and source
  checks;
- completing one candidate reward-test packet that rejects itself as premature
  if required evidence is missing;
- or separating one transfer context into source evidence, target evidence,
  capture pressure, and public-language boundaries.

Unsafe local work includes:

- adopting a phase, power, emergency rule, rollback rule, appeal path, or
  reviewer-authority model;
- treating retained records, loss notes, scores, or review states as reward,
  ownership, token, legal, financial, employment, or governance claims;
- limiting contributors, requiring disclosures, defining sanctions, or changing
  eligibility rules as a capture response;
- making AI output the final review reason, score, novelty judgment, or policy
  basis;
- choosing transfer pilots or publishing generalization language;
- or marking second-ring tests, claims, or research questions as passed.

## Dependency Order For Later Review

A later human review can read second-ring artifacts in this order:

1. Start with trace preservation: RQ5, T3, T10, and T11 show whether the source
   workflow creates visible contribution records.
2. Read RQ6 next: governance movement depends on named powers and evidence
   tiers.
3. Read RQ7 before RQ9: rights and retained-record boundaries must stay separate
   from reward logic.
4. Read RQ8 before any adoption decision: capture pressure changes the meaning
   of governance, reward, AI, and transfer choices.
5. Read C7/T8 wherever AI appears in evidence review, scoring, prior-art work,
   or rationale drafting.
6. Read RQ9 only after the no-reward baseline and attack delta are visible.
7. Read RQ10 last: transfer should consume earlier gates, not route around them.

This order is a review aid, not an adopted governance procedure.

## Early Red Flags

Treat second-ring work as blocked, not merely incomplete, if:

- a decision needs private founder memory to explain why a contribution record
  is legitimate;
- a governance move names a phase but not the power being transferred;
- emergency language lacks scope, expiration, contestability, or preservation of
  affected records;
- retained-value language sounds like reward, ownership, debt, or governance
  weight;
- capture response would reject, sanction, disclose, identify, or limit a real
  contributor without adopted policy;
- AI-generated synthesis hides the human rationale or source trail;
- a reward test cannot state its no-reward baseline;
- transfer would require saying the pattern works before source traces and
  target evidence exist;
- or the next action would create non-GitHub external consequences.

## Minimum Second-Ring Review Packet

Before a later review treats any second-ring question as ready for decision, the
packet should include:

1. the exact research question and affected power, right, reward alternative,
   capture response, AI role, or transfer object;
2. source artifacts that make the evidence inspectable without private context;
3. the dependency gates already satisfied and the gates still blocked;
4. one adverse or contested trace if the decision affects contributor records;
5. one capture-risk note and one overclaim-risk note;
6. the public-language boundary that prevents premature claims;
7. the Joe/governance review point;
8. and the stop condition that would abandon, narrow, or defer the decision.
