Status: review-prep scaffold, not adopted governance.

Purpose: make emergency and rollback authority reviewable before the repo adopts any rule that would temporarily concentrate power, reverse a governance experiment, pause participation, or correct a harmful public record.

## Boundary Under Review

RQ6 needs a way to distinguish legitimate temporary correction from permanent hidden control.

For this scaffold, an emergency or rollback action is reviewable only if the record can answer:

1. What condition triggered the action?
2. Which authority was temporarily concentrated or restored?
3. Which contribution records, review states, or governance powers were affected?
4. What is the narrowest scope that could address the harm?
5. What expires the action?
6. Who can contest the action or request a review note?
7. What evidence would show the action became capture rather than protection?

Those are review questions, not adopted procedural rules.

## Trigger Boundary Matrix

| candidate trigger | possible temporary action | minimum record needed | legitimacy condition tested | capture risk if vague |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Public record error | Append correction, pause reliance on a record, or route to later review. | Original record, error claim, affected field, correction rationale, and unresolved uncertainty. | Visibility, adaptive rule change | Corrections can become silent revision of history. |
| Contribution-spam flood | Slow intake, batch triage, or mark duplicate submissions. | Volume signal, affected contribution class, triage rule used, and duration. | Bounded authority, non-capture | Anti-spam language can suppress inconvenient but valid critique. |
| Adversarial coordination | Require extra evidence, conflict disclosure, or reviewer separation. | Pattern evidence, affected review path, conflict concern, and challenge route. | Non-capture, contestability | Suspicion can become identity-based gatekeeping. |
| Reviewer conflict or breakdown | Pause a decision, add a second reviewer later, or preserve disputed status. | Conflict signal, decision affected, reviewer boundary, and next review condition. | Contestability, bounded authority | Founder can use conflict claims to reclaim all decisions. |
| Reward or retained-value leakage | Add non-promissory clarification or halt reward-like interpretation. | Language at issue, possible entitlement reading, affected records, and adoption stop. | Visibility, non-capture | Clarification can erase contributor expectations without preserving loss. |
| Public posture overclaim | Add a draft-status note or route to governance review. | Overclaim location, claim boundary, affected audience, and corrective text. | Visibility, adaptive rule change | Posture correction can become reputation management rather than truth repair. |
| Phase-transition failure | Revert a pilot, narrow transferred power, or return a decision to founder-led review. | Power transferred, failure signal, affected contributors, and rollback review point. | Bounded authority, non-capture | Rollback can make transfer reversible only when founder dislikes outcomes. |
| Contributor safety or privacy issue | Temporarily hide or narrow an identifier while preserving record integrity. | Affected identifier, safety concern, record-preservation plan, and review condition. | Exit with retained record, visibility | Safety language can hide accountability or rewrite attribution. |
| Legal or external-consequence concern | Stop before action and require explicit Joe/professional review. | Concern type, affected surface, and exact external consequence risk. | Bounded authority | Legal concern can become a universal veto over uncomfortable work. |

## Rollback Types To Keep Separate

| rollback type | what changes | what must remain visible | should not imply |
|---|---|---|---|
| Record correction | A specific artifact, field, rationale, or link is corrected or annotated. | The original issue, correction rationale, and remaining uncertainty. | That the original contribution never happened. |
| Review-state rollback | A contribution returns from `accepted`, `needs_revision`, `not_accepted`, or `contested` to a prior review state. | Prior state, reason for rollback, affected contributor, and next review condition. | That reviewers can erase adverse evidence. |
| Policy-draft rollback | A draft rule, rubric, cadence, or phase criterion is narrowed or withdrawn. | The draft status, reason, and affected research question. | That the repo silently changed active policy. |
| Authority rollback | A transferred or piloted power returns to founder-led review or a narrower reviewer set. | The power, transfer evidence, failure signal, and expiration/review point. | That all future transfer is illegitimate. |
| Public-posture rollback | A public-facing claim, framing, or status note is demoted or clarified. | The original overclaim risk and the corrected boundary. | That the project is hiding failure rather than recording it. |
| Emergency pause | A workflow, review lane, or contribution class is temporarily paused. | The trigger, scope, owner, expiry condition, and contest path. | That indefinite suspension is normal governance. |

## Minimum Emergency Record

A later emergency or rollback procedure should be able to produce a compact record with:

- trigger and source surface;
- affected power, workflow, contribution record, or public claim;
- current phase and authority holder;
- narrow temporary action;
- rationale and evidence;
- scope limit;
- expiration condition;
- contestability or review route;
- contributor-record preservation note;
- capture-risk note;
- follow-up owner or review date;
- and explicit non-adoption language if the action is still draft or pilot-only.

This packet shape is a candidate review object. It does not authorize creating live emergency records yet.

## Expiration And Review Conditions

Emergency authority should be treated as suspect unless the later adopted rule can name at least one concrete exit condition.

Candidate expiration conditions to test:

- the malformed record is corrected or annotated;
- the spam or adversarial pattern is no longer active;
- the affected contributor has a chance to contest or clarify;
- a second reviewer or Joe/governance review resolves the disputed state;
- the draft public-posture language is corrected;
- a pilot power is narrowed or re-authorized with fresh evidence;
- or the action reaches a named time or event boundary and must be renewed explicitly.

Open-ended emergency language is evidence of governance risk, not maturity.

## Failure Modes

| failure mode | early warning | possible mitigation to test |
|---|---|---|
| Permanent emergency | The action has no expiry condition or renewal record. | Require trigger, scope, and expiration in the same record. |
| Founder veto laundering | Emergency language reverses an outcome because the founder dislikes it. | Name the affected power and evidence standard before rollback. |
| Contributor erasure | Correction or rollback removes accepted work or useful loss notes. | Append correction where possible; preserve the record and disputed state. |
| Identity gatekeeping | Adversarial-risk language becomes a proxy for credentials or status. | Separate behavior evidence from contributor identity and preserve open attemptability. |
| Appeal theater | A contest route exists but cannot change the record or prompt review. | Record what contestability can actually do at the current phase. |
| Reward panic | Any reward-adjacent ambiguity triggers broad suppression of contribution value. | Clarify non-promissory language without erasing value or loss. |
| Public-image cleanup | Rollback hides failure to preserve reputation. | Treat negative results and overclaim corrections as visible learning artifacts. |
| Transfer chilling effect | A failed pilot becomes evidence that no power should ever move. | Keep rollback power-specific and record what would make a later pilot safer. |

## Later Review Prompts

1. Which exact authority is being concentrated, paused, restored, or rolled back?
2. What harm would occur if no temporary action happened?
3. What is the smallest intervention that preserves record integrity?
4. How does the affected contributor or participant see and contest the action?
5. What evidence would prove the action should expire?
6. What record remains if the action is later judged mistaken?
7. Does the rollback reduce capture risk, or merely restore founder preference?
8. Does the action create reward, rights, legal, or public-posture meaning that requires Joe review?
